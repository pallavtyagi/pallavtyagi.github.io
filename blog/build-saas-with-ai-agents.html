Build a SaaS With AI Agents: A Weekend Story

From Python scripts and S3 parsing to an AI-native email platform in 48 hours — four agent teams, nine architecture diagrams, and an MCP connector that lets AI build your templates.

TL;DR: A tight deadline forced me to hack together a Python script for bulk email. A week later, I replaced it with a full SaaS — built in one weekend using 4 parallel AI agent teams in Claude Code. The platform lets you build templates with in-app AI or push them from Claude via an MCP connector. GitHub Research shows AI-assisted developers complete tasks 55% faster (GitHub Research, 2022). The real unlock was system design first, agents second.

Friday evening I had a problem. Sunday night I had a deployed product. A week earlier, I'd needed to send thousands of emails on a tight deadline and there was nothing in the toolbox to deliver it. No internal tool, no budget for an enterprise platform, no time to evaluate vendors. So I did what developers do — I hacked together a Python script. It called Amazon SES to send, piped events into Kinesis Data Firehose, dumped them into S3, and then I ran another script to manually pull and parse those event files to figure out what actually happened. No UI. No dedup. No real-time anything. Just scripts, cron jobs, and prayer.

And here's the thing — this isn't a niche problem. Every team that outgrows Mailchimp but doesn't want to run Salesforce Marketing Cloud hits this wall. They need a tool simple enough that AI can help with the hard parts: building templates inside the tool itself, or crafting them in Claude and pushing them through an MCP connector. That's the product I set out to build.

So I built a micro-SaaS email platform using four parallel AI agent teams through Claude Code. The DORA 2025 Report found that 90% of developers now use AI tools in some capacity. Most use them for autocomplete suggestions. I used them as an entire engineering organisation. One weekend. Twenty-eight tasks. Four agents. Nine architecture diagrams. And one very sleep-deprived human making all the decisions.

Why Did I Need to Build My Own Email Tool?

A week before the weekend build, I had a deadline and nothing in the toolbox to meet it. I needed to send thousands of emails with tracking and deduplication. No internal tool existed. The enterprise platforms needed weeks of procurement. So I built a Python script.

Here's what I actually needed:

  • Resilience — retries, error handling, never losing an email in the queue
  • Deduplication — never send the same email twice to the same recipient
  • Speed — deliver thousands of emails within minutes, not hours
  • Tracking — delivery confirmations, bounce handling, opens, clicks, engagement metrics

The Python script I'd thrown together the week before got the job done — barely. It called SES to send, Kinesis Data Firehose captured events, and everything landed as JSON files in S3. To see what bounced or who opened what, I ran a separate parsing script against those S3 objects — manually, on my laptop. There was no dedup protection. One morning I discovered 200 emails had gone out twice because a retry loop fired without checking what had already been sent. That was the breaking point.

The existing tools sat at two extremes. Amazon SES gives you raw sending power but zero campaign management — no UI, no scheduling, no analytics dashboard. Platforms like Mailchimp and SendGrid are designed for marketing teams, not developers who want fine-grained control. And their pricing gets ugly fast once you cross a few thousand sends.

This is a common problem. Every developer, startup, or small team that needs to send emails at scale eventually builds some version of my Python-and-S3 contraption. They all hit the same walls: no dedup, no real-time tracking, and templates that are either hand-coded HTML or copy-pasted from a design tool. The insight that turned this from “yet another SES wrapper” into something worth building: what if the platform was simple enough that AI could handle the hard parts? Build your template right inside the tool with AI assistance — or craft it in Claude, ChatGPT, or any other AI tool and push it into the platform through an MCP connector. The email tool becomes AI-native infrastructure, not just a sending pipe.

The common problem: Stack Overflow's 2025 Developer Survey found that 84% of developers use or plan to use AI tools, up from 76% in 2024. The real gap isn't “sending emails” — it's having a tool simple enough that AI can plug into it, either generating templates in-app or connecting externally via MCP.

What Exactly Did I Set Out to Build?

A study from PwC (2025) found that 66% of companies adopting AI agents report increased productivity. I wanted to apply that productivity to a very specific product: a micro-SaaS that processes bulk email campaigns end-to-end with resilience, deduplication, and real-time tracking baked into the core.

The Core Promise

Send thousands of emails without losing any, without sending duplicates, and with real-time visibility into what happened to every single message. But more than that: make the tool AI-friendly from the ground up. Build your email template inside the app with AI assistance, or build it in Claude and push it straight into the platform via MCP. No more copying HTML between tools. No more manual anything.

The Key Features

  • Bulk sending at scale — a job queue with rate limiting, batched delivery, and a warm-up schedule that ramps from 50 emails per day to 200,000 over 14 days
  • Recipient deduplication — database-level uniqueness constraints prevent double-sends
  • Real-time tracking — delivery, bounces (hard and soft), opens, clicks, and unsubscribes streamed from SES events
  • Custom domain sending — SPF, DKIM, and DMARC verification on your own domains
  • AI template generation in-app — describe your brand and the built-in AI generates complete email templates, subject lines, and body copy
  • MCP connector — build your template in Claude, ChatGPT, or any MCP-compatible tool, then push it directly into the platform via structured tool calls
  • Drag-and-drop editor — an Unlayer-based template editor for manual tweaks, no HTML knowledge required

How Did I Design the Entire System on a Friday Evening?

Only 11% of organisations actively use agentic AI in production workflows (Deloitte Tech Trends 2026). A big reason? Most teams skip design and jump straight to prompting. With AI agents, the requirements document isn't just helpful — it's the entire foundation.

The PRD Session: Specific Constraints, Not Vague Prompts

Friday at 7pm, I sat down with Claude and wrote the Product Requirements Document. Not “write me a PRD for an email platform” — that produces generic output. I fed in specific constraints drawn from my actual pain:

  • The problem statement: I need to send thousands of emails with retries, dedup, and tracking
  • The market gap: Between developer-raw (SES SDK) and marketing-bloated (Mailchimp)
  • The core user loop: Verify domain, import contacts, build template (with AI), send campaign, track results
  • Non-negotiables: Custom domain sending, AI template generation via MCP, workspace-scoped multi-tenancy
  • Scale targets: 50 emails per day on day one, ramping to 200,000 per day over a 14-day warm-up

Writing requirements for AI agents requires 3–4x more specificity than writing for human developers. Humans fill gaps with intuition and questions at standup. Agents fill gaps with assumptions — often wrong ones. I spent almost three hours on the PRD and system design. That time paid for itself tenfold over the weekend.

The Nine Architecture Diagrams

With the PRD locked, I used Claude to generate a comprehensive system design document. Nine architecture diagrams came out of that session, each addressing a specific question the agents would face while building.

  1. High-Level Architecture — Three columns: Next.js frontend on Vercel, Fastify API on Railway (with PostgreSQL and Redis), and external services (SES, SNS, S3, Claude API). Clean separation, no frontend-to-database shortcuts.
  2. Email Sending Pipeline — The heart of the product. User hits “Send,” API creates a campaign record, BullMQ batches and rate-limits delivery, warm-up throttle controls velocity from 50 to 200,000 emails per day over 14 days.
  3. SES Event Tracking — SES fires events to an SNS topic, which webhooks into the Fastify API. Real-time, scalable to thousands of events per second, zero polling required.
  4. Domain Verification Flow — User submits domain, API creates SES identity, SES returns DNS records (SPF, DKIM, DMARC), background worker polls until verified.
  5. Campaign State Machine — DRAFT → SCHEDULED → SENDING → SENT, with branches to PAUSED or CANCELLED. API enforces valid transitions.
  6. Auth and Multi-Tenancy — Clerk organisations map to workspace IDs. Every query filtered by workspace. Role-based access: Owners, Admins, Marketers, Analysts.
  7. AI Features Architecture — Two paths: in-app AI generation through the Claude API (subject lines, copywriting, full templates), and an MCP server that lets external AI tools push templates and content directly into the platform.
  8. CI/CD Flow — Three parallel GitHub Actions workflows: frontend to Vercel, API to Railway, Terraform to AWS. Path filters prevent unnecessary deploys.
  9. Database Schema — Sixteen models, eight enums. Full lifecycle from domain verification through send jobs to tracked events.

Why so much design upfront? According to Deloitte's Tech Trends 2026 report, only 11% of organisations actively use agentic AI in production. The biggest barrier isn't technology — it's the requirement specification gap. Agent teams need system design documents that are two to three times more detailed than what human teams typically produce.

How Did the AI Agents Actually Build It?

GitHub Copilot research shows AI-assisted developers complete tasks 55% faster (GitHub Research, n=95, 2022). But Copilot gives you one fast pair of hands. I needed four specialists working simultaneously. Here's how I gave each agent the context it needed to build independently.

How Each Agent Got Its Context

Each agent ran as a separate Claude Code session with a focused system prompt. I didn't just say “build the frontend.” Each agent received:

  1. The full system design doc — so it understood the entire architecture
  2. Its specific task list — with exact acceptance criteria
  3. The shared package types — so API contracts matched across agents
  4. Hard constraints — “Use Fastify 5.1, not Express. Use shadcn/ui, not Material UI. Use Prisma 6.1, not Drizzle.”

The constraints matter more than you'd think. Without them, one agent might choose Express while another builds Fastify middleware. Explicit constraints prevent entire categories of integration bugs.

Parallel Execution in Practice

While the AI Engineer was building the Claude API service layer, the Lead Architect was simultaneously writing Terraform for the email infrastructure. Neither needed the other's output. They shared types from the packages/shared directory, and that was enough.

The AI service class is a good example of the quality agents produce when given clear constraints:

// packages/ai/src/ai-service.ts
export class AIService {
  private client: Anthropic;
  private model: string;

  constructor(config: AIServiceConfig) {
    this.client = new Anthropic({
      apiKey: config.apiKey,
      maxRetries: config.maxRetries ?? 3,
      timeout: config.timeout ?? 30_000,
    });
    this.model = config.model ?? 'claude-sonnet-4-20250514';
  }

  async generateText(
    systemPrompt: string,
    userPrompt: string
  ): Promise<string> {
    const response = await this.client.messages.create({
      model: this.model,
      max_tokens: 4096,
      system: systemPrompt,
      messages: [{ role: 'user', content: userPrompt }],
    });
    const textBlock = response.content.find(b => b.type === 'text');
    if (!textBlock || textBlock.type !== 'text') {
      throw new Error('No text response from AI');
    }
    return textBlock.text;
  }
}

This powers one half of the AI story: in-app template generation where users describe their brand and the AI builds everything. The other half is the MCP connector — an MCP server that exposes the platform's template and campaign APIs as tools. A user working in Claude can say “create a welcome email for new signups in a friendly tone” and the AI pushes a finished template directly into the platform. No copying. No exporting. The email tool becomes a tool that AI uses, not just a tool humans click through.

Turborepo as the Coordination Layer

The monorepo's build system acted as an invisible coordination layer. When Turborepo type-checked the frontend, it automatically built the shared types package first. This caught mismatches between agent outputs early. If the Backend agent defined a campaign status as a string enum and the Frontend agent expected different values, the build would fail before anything shipped.

How Did I Test the Product With Claude in Chrome?

Rakuten achieved 99.9% accuracy on autonomous code modifications across a 12.5-million-line codebase in 7 hours using AI agents (Anthropic, 2026). My scope was smaller, but the approach was more unusual: I used Claude directly in Chrome as an AI QA tester, navigating the live application and verifying that everything actually worked before writing a single automated test.

Claude as QA Tester in the Browser

After the agents finished building the code, I didn't just run npm run dev and click around myself. I had Claude open the application in Chrome and walk through every major user flow. An AI system acting as a manual QA tester — not generating test scripts, but actually loading pages, inspecting the DOM, and verifying that interactive elements worked.

Here's what Claude tested in the browser:

  • Authentication Flow — Clerk widget rendered, redirect logic worked, dev-mode dashboard access verified
  • Dashboard — Sidebar navigation, workspace header, summary cards, and navigation links all rendered correctly
  • Domain Management — “Add Domain” button present and clickable, table structure correct
  • Contact Management — Contact list with expected columns, CSV import flow accessible
  • Campaign Creation — Multi-step wizard rendered, form elements interactive, step navigation worked
  • Template Editor — Unlayer drag-and-drop editor initialised correctly (a misconfigured integration shows a blank space)
  • Analytics Dashboard — Metric labels appeared — total sent, delivered, bounced, opened, clicked

From Manual Testing to Automated Specs

After Claude validated flows manually, the AI Engineer agent generated six Playwright spec files covering the full user lifecycle: auth, domains, contacts, campaigns, templates, and analytics. The Playwright config used Chromium with auto-starting dev server, parallel execution, and retry logic for flaky tests in CI.

What would I do differently? Write tests alongside each feature, not as a final task. When tests come last, they're thin because you're racing the clock. When they come alongside, each agent writes tests for its own feature while the context is fresh.

What Does the Git History Reveal About AI Agent Development?

The Stack Overflow 2025 Survey found that 45.2% of developers say debugging AI-generated code takes longer than debugging code they wrote themselves. My git history confirms this — and reveals a three-phase pattern that I believe is universal for agent-built projects.

Phase 1: The Velocity Rush (Saturday)

The first four commits are massive feature output. The entire platform scaffolded, features built, editor integrated. The agents are building independently and shipping fast. This is where the “AI is amazing” feeling peaks.

Phase 2: The Valley of Integration Pain (Saturday Night to Sunday Morning)

Commits five through fifteen. TypeScript errors. CI configuration fixes. Deploy failures. Platform-specific debugging. This is the 2am grind — the phase where projects die if you don't expect it.

Phase 3: Stabilise and Ship (Sunday Afternoon)

The final commits. Hard infrastructure problems are solved. Now it's UI refinement, documentation, and the landing page. This phase feels calm because you've already survived the valley.

The pattern: Big commits early, fix clusters in the middle, polish at the end. Understanding this curve — and budgeting for it — separates a successful agent-built project from an abandoned one.

What Were the Biggest Lessons From This Weekend?

According to Gartner (2025), 40% of enterprise apps will use task-specific AI agents by end of 2026, up from under 5% in 2025. That trajectory is clear. But adoption without understanding the failure modes is a recipe for frustration.

Lesson 1: The Requirements Doc Is Your Entire Foundation

With human developers, you can get away with vague specs because they'll ask questions at standup. Agents won't interrupt you. They'll make assumptions and keep building confidently. I spent Friday evening — almost three hours — on the PRD and system design. That was the highest-ROI time of the entire weekend.

Lesson 2: Shared Types Prevent Weekend-Killing Bugs

The shared types package — Zod schemas, TypeScript types, constants — was the glue between agents. When the Frontend agent imported a campaign status type, it matched what the Backend agent's API returned. Without that shared contract, I'd still be debugging mismatched field names.

Lesson 3: CI/CD Will Humble You

Agents produce code that's syntactically correct but environmentally naive. They don't know your Vercel project isn't linked, your Railway service name changed, or your Terraform state already has existing resources. Budget at least 20% of your time for deploy fixes.

Lesson 4: Context Window Management Is a Real Skill

By Sunday, some tasks needed context from five or six files across the monorepo. Giving each agent exact file paths and function signatures — not just vague descriptions — dramatically improved output quality. apps/api/src/routes/campaigns.ts beats “the campaign API” every single time.

Lesson 5: You're Still the Bottleneck

The agents produced code faster than I could review it. The Prisma schema alone is 462 lines across 16 models and 8 enums. Rushing through review is how you ship bugs to production. That Faros AI finding about 91% more review time? I felt every percentage point of it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a solo developer really build a SaaS in one weekend with AI agents?

Yes, with major caveats. You need strong architecture skills to define the work upfront, and you should expect a brutal middle phase of integration debugging. GitHub Research found AI-assisted developers complete tasks 55% faster (GitHub Research, 2022). Agent teams amplify this with parallelism. But the weekend includes sleep deprivation and the 2am CI/CD fixes.

What makes the MCP connector approach different from regular AI features?

Most email tools bolt AI on as a sidebar feature. This platform works two ways: in-app AI generates templates directly inside the editor, and an MCP server exposes the platform's APIs as tools that external AI can call. That means you can build a template in Claude, ChatGPT, or any MCP-compatible tool and push it straight into the platform — no copying, no exporting. The email tool becomes infrastructure that AI plugs into.

How much did the AI tooling and infrastructure cost?

Claude Code was used for all four agent sessions. Infrastructure costs — Railway starter tier, Vercel free tier, AWS SES at pennies per thousand emails — were minimal. Total weekend spend on AI and infrastructure was significantly less than one day of contract developer rates.

What's the biggest risk of using AI agent teams?

Integration bugs at the boundaries between agents. The Frontend agent might expect emailCount while the Backend agent returns email_count. Shared types in a monorepo package mitigate this but don't eliminate it. Budget 20–30% of your time for integration work.

Do AI agents replace the need for software architecture skills?

Not even close. Agents amplify existing skills, they don't substitute for them. You still need to design data models, choose infrastructure, structure dependencies, and make hard tradeoff decisions. The DORA 2025 Report shows 90% of developers use AI tools — but the gap between architects who guide agents with detailed specs and developers who prompt blindly is enormous. The AI built what I designed. It didn't design anything on its own.
