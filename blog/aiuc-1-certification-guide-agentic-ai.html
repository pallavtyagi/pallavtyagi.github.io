AIUC-1 Certification: What It Takes to Certify Your AI Agent

The first security and safety standard built for agentic AI — six domains, four phases, and everything your team needs to know before starting the audit.

TL;DR: AIUC-1 is the world's first certification standard purpose-built for AI agents, covering security, safety, reliability, data privacy, accountability, and societal impact. Backed by a $15M seed round and 100+ consortium members, the 4-8 week audit process tests your agent against thousands of adversarial scenarios. Five companies are already certified, and enterprise buyers are increasingly asking for it.

What Is AIUC-1 and Why Should You Care?

The AIUC-1 Consortium launched in November 2025 and has grown to 100+ members including Fortune 500 CISOs, collectively handling tens of trillions of dollars in payments (AIUC-1 Consortium, 2026). AIUC-1 is the world's first independent standard for verifying the security, safety, and reliability of AI agents — and it's quickly becoming the trust signal that enterprise buyers look for before deploying autonomous AI.

Think of it as SOC 2 for AI agents. Where SOC 2 validates your cloud security controls, AIUC-1 validates that your AI agent won't hallucinate dangerous outputs, leak customer data, or execute tool calls it shouldn't. Having spent years building AI governance frameworks in regulated financial services, I can tell you this is the kind of standard the industry has been waiting for.

The standard was created by the Artificial Intelligence Underwriting Company (AIUC), founded by Rune Kvist (first product hire at Anthropic), Brandon Wang (Thiel Fellow), and Rajiv Dattani (former McKinsey partner). It emerged from stealth in July 2025 with a $15 million seed round led by Nat Friedman, former CEO of GitHub (Fortune, 2025).

What Makes AIUC-1 Different from Existing AI Frameworks?

Existing frameworks like ISO 42001, NIST AI RMF, and the EU AI Act provide broad governance guidelines, but none address the specific risks of autonomous AI agents that invoke tools, access sensitive data, and act on behalf of users. AIUC-1 fills that gap with testable, auditable controls.

AIUC-1 doesn't replace SOC 2 or ISO 27001. It operationalises frameworks like MITRE ATLAS, OWASP Top 10 for LLMs, and Cisco's AI Security Framework into concrete, testable requirements for your agent. I covered this in detail on my AI Signals podcast episode on AIUC-1.

What Are the Six AIUC-1 Domains?

The standard tests your AI agent across controls organised into six domains. The exact number of controls depends on your agent's risk profile — a more capable agent with multimodal inputs, tool call access, or sensitive data handling faces a higher evidence bar (AIUC-1 Certification, 2026).

1. Data & Privacy

Controls that prevent data leakage, IP theft, and cross-customer data exposure. Your agent must have explicit policies on data collection, processing, retention, and deletion. PII and IP leakage assessments are mandatory.

2. Security

Covers adversarial attacks, jailbreaks, prompt injection, and endpoint scraping. You'll need third-party adversarial robustness testing, rate limiting, query quota enforcement, encryption, and access controls.

3. Safety

Ensures your agent doesn't produce harmful, out-of-scope, biased, or deceptive outputs. You must define application-specific risk taxonomies and run pre-deployment testing for harmful content, hallucinations, and policy violations.

4. Reliability

Targets hallucinated outputs and unsafe tool calls. Hallucination prevention testing is mandatory every 3 months. Tool call authorisation and scope validation are required — your agent must never execute actions it isn't explicitly authorised to perform.

5. Accountability

Requires documented failure plans, formal approval processes, and vendor due diligence. There must be clear governance structures for AI system changes throughout the lifecycle, with defined ownership and escalation paths.

6. Society

Addresses AI-enabled cyberattacks and the potential for catastrophic misuse. Mandatory guardrails against cyber exploitation and system misuse must be in place to prevent your agent from being weaponised.

How Does the Certification Process Work?

The certification involves thousands of adversarial scenarios derived from real-world incidents, with quarterly re-testing to ensure ongoing compliance (Schellman, 2026). The process runs across four phases over 4-8 weeks.

Phase 1: Scoping and Kick-off (1-2 weeks)

Define the product scope, assign your internal team, and identify evidence gaps. The accredited auditor works with you to document the audit scope and flag any blockers early.

Phase 2: Evidence Gathering (3-5 weeks)

This is the heaviest lift. You'll collect and document operational practices, legal and governance policies, and technical implementation details. Evidence falls into four categories: legal policies, technical implementation, operational practices, and third-party evaluations.

Phase 3: Technical Testing (1-3 weeks)

The adversarial testing phase. Red-team exercises simulate real-world attacks: prompt injection, data leakage scenarios, jailbreak attempts, and hallucination stress tests. Scenarios are modelled on documented real-world AI failures.

Phase 4: Audit Finalisation

Evidence is combined into a final report. Any P0 or P1 vulnerabilities discovered during testing must be remediated before the certificate is issued. Once signed off, you receive the audit report, certificate, and the AIUC-1 badge.

Key difference from SOC 2: AIUC-1 is forward-looking (adversarial testing against what could go wrong) rather than backward-looking (did your controls hold over the past year). Re-testing is quarterly, not annual.

What Technical Evidence Do You Need to Prepare?

Organisations with mature AI governance complete the process in 5-10 weeks (CompliancePoint, 2026). The controls scale with capability — a more powerful agent with multimodal inputs, tool call access, or sensitive data access faces a higher evidence bar.

Evidence Category What You Need Examples
Legal Policies Data handling, privacy, terms of use Data retention policy, DPA, privacy notice
Technical Implementation Architecture docs, security controls Encryption at rest/transit, access control lists, rate limiting config
Operational Practices Processes and runbooks Incident response plan, model change approval workflow, monitoring dashboards
Third-Party Evaluations Independent testing results Penetration test reports, adversarial robustness assessments, bias audits

The Q1 2026 update added requirements for threat modelling in pre-deployment testing, stronger PII protection in logs, multimodal coverage of AI labelling, and voice agent security requirements developed with Stanford University and ElevenLabs (AIUC Q1 2026 Update).

Who Has Already Been Certified?

Five companies have publicly announced AIUC-1 certification as of March 2026. The first certifications were issued in December 2025 — just five months after AIUC emerged from stealth.

How Does the Insurance Component Work?

AIUC offers liability coverage for AI agent failures, including hallucinations, IP infringement, and data leakage. The insurance component is optional and separate from the certification itself (Fortune, 2025). ElevenLabs was the first company to secure AIUC-1-backed insurance.

The model works as a trifecta: standards + audits + insurance. The AIUC-1 framework sets the baseline. Independent audits validate real-world agent behaviour. Insurance pricing reflects your system's safety posture — a better audit result means better premiums.

For enterprise buyers evaluating AI vendors, this creates a clear signal. A certified agent with insurance coverage reduces procurement risk significantly.

How Does AIUC-1 Apply to Your Agentic AI Application?

Enterprise adoption of agentic AI is accelerating rapidly. AIUC was created with 100+ Fortune 500 CISOs specifically to unlock this adoption by providing the trust infrastructure enterprises require (AIUC-1, 2026). As more organisations deploy autonomous agents, certification becomes a competitive differentiator.

AIUC-1 is relevant if your AI agent does any of the following:

  • Invokes external tools — API calls, database queries, file system access
  • Acts autonomously — makes decisions without human approval for each action
  • Handles sensitive data — PII, financial records, health information
  • Interacts with end users — customer service, sales, support
  • Operates in regulated industries — finance, healthcare, legal

The standard uses capability-based scoping, introduced in Q1 2026. An agent with multimodal inputs/outputs, tool call access, or sensitive data access faces a higher evidence bar than a simple text-in, text-out assistant. In my experience building agentic AI frameworks at Barrenjoey, the tool call authorisation and data access controls are where most teams have the biggest gaps.

What Does the Ongoing Commitment Look Like?

Certification isn't a one-time exercise. AIUC-1 requires continuous compliance with quarterly re-testing and annual re-certification (AIUC-1 Certificate, 2026).

Requirement Frequency Details
Adversarial re-testing Every 3 months (minimum) Hallucination, prompt injection, tool call validation
Standard updates Quarterly (Jan, Apr, Jul, Oct) New controls may be added; existing controls may be strengthened
Full re-certification Annual Complete audit of all technical, operational, and legal controls
Vulnerability remediation As discovered P0/P1 issues must be resolved to maintain certification status

The standard itself is a living document updated on a predictable quarterly cadence. This means your compliance programme needs to evolve with it.

How to Get Started: A Practical Checklist

If you're considering AIUC-1 for your agentic AI application, here's a practical starting point:

  1. Audit your current state — Map your agent's capabilities (tool calls, data access, autonomy level) against the six domains
  2. Document your governance — Write up data handling policies, incident response plans, and model change approval workflows
  3. Run internal adversarial tests — Test for prompt injection, hallucination rates, and tool call boundary violations before engaging an auditor
  4. Engage an accredited auditor — Schellman is currently the first and primary accredited AIUC-1 auditor
  5. Plan for quarterly cadence — Budget for re-testing every 3 months and track the quarterly standard updates

Pricing note: AIUC-1 certification costs are not publicly disclosed. Contact Schellman or AIUC directly via their intake form for a custom quote based on your agent's complexity and risk profile.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is AIUC-1 certification?

AIUC-1 is the world's first independent security, safety, and reliability standard designed specifically for AI agents. It provides third-party verification across six domains: Data & Privacy, Security, Safety, Reliability, Accountability, and Society. Think of it as SOC 2 for AI agents.

How long does AIUC-1 certification take?

The full certification process takes 4-8 weeks across four phases: scoping and kick-off (1-2 weeks), evidence gathering (3-5 weeks), technical testing (1-3 weeks), and audit finalisation. Organisations with mature AI governance practices may complete it in 5-10 weeks.

How much does AIUC-1 certification cost?

Pricing is not publicly disclosed. Contact an accredited auditor (currently Schellman) or AIUC directly for a custom quote. Cost varies based on the scope, complexity, and risk profile of your AI agent.

Is AIUC-1 an individual exam or an organisational certification?

AIUC-1 is an organisational and product certification, not an individual professional credential. There is no exam for individuals to take. It certifies that an AI agent system meets rigorous standards across security, safety, reliability, and governance.

Which companies have achieved AIUC-1 certification?

As of March 2026, certified companies include Intercom (customer service AI), Recraft (AI image generation), ElevenLabs (voice AI), Ada (AI customer service platform), and UiPath (enterprise automation). The AIUC-1 Consortium has 100+ members including Fortune 500 CISOs.

Does AIUC-1 replace SOC 2 or ISO 27001?

No. AIUC-1 complements existing certifications. It covers AI-specific risks that SOC 2 and ISO 27001 don't address, such as hallucinations, prompt injection, unsafe tool calls, and agent-specific data leakage. You still need to maintain independent compliance with those standards.
